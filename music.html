I'm a big classical nerd.

Along with my passion for physics and mathematics, I have always been a highly musical person. I've been playing piano most of my life, and cello for a little less time. At the end of my first year at UCSB, I assembled a casual string quartet to play through the standard repertoire (our favorites include Borodin and Mendelssohn). Recently, I have been doing the same with the piano trio repertoire (currently my favorite chamber ensemble, we enjoy Schubert and Mendelssohn). During my junior year, I was an officer of a club bringing music to community centers in Santa Barbara, including behavioral health centers, homeless shelters, retirement homes, etc. I have been a cellist in the UCSB Pops orchestra for the past 3 years. My interest in music comes from both performance and history.

I have always indulged in exploring music history. The stories of composers inform their music, and I find the interconnected nature of classical music through the past several hundred years exciting. A treat for the reader - I discovered one historical gem this summer; as Tchaikovsky wrote an elegy trio to Nikolai Rubinstein, as Rachmaninoff wrote an elegy trio to Tchaikovsky, the story went one level deeper. A more obscure and sparsely recorded Russian composer, Alexander Goldenweiser, wrote an elegy trio for Rachmaninoff, completing a tragically beautiful trio of elegy trios.

I have more Rachmaninoff days than anything else. Some of my favorite pieces as of right now are his second piano sonata, and his second trio elegiaque. I don't neccesarily like to be sad, but I cant help myself with Rachmaninoff. Recently, I've also been obsessed with Schumann's Symphonic Etudes and Medtner's piano quintet. For the cello, I have not explored the standard repertoire as much, although some of my favorite pieces are Max Bruch's Kol Nidrei and the prelude to Bach's 4th cello suite.

Recently, I've been trying to play jazz.

I realized how unhealthy it is to only play classical...although I have to say the background in theory and history I got from being stuck in 1850s Vienna really does give me a great appreciation for jazz. After taking a vernacular harmony course in my first year at UCSB, I made it my goal to become proficient in this new genre.

Learning to improvise is extremely rewarding and entertaining. It's funny to see myself play something and be taken aback by the interesting statements I can sometimes realize. It would be nice to find some other players at my level (more or less) to explore this with.
